Ancient/Medieval Church Map:
Spiritual Interpretation

  • Orienting tool: spiritual interpretation
  • Map purpose: to overcome discontinuity between the OT and NT

Origen (ca. 185–254)

  • “As man consists of body, and soul, and spirit, so in the same way does Scripture, which has been arranged to be given by God for the salvation of men.” (De princ. 4.1.11)
  • “But as there are certain passages of Scripture which do not at all contain the ‘corporeal’ sense, … there are also places where we must seek only for the ‘soul,’ as it were, and ‘spirit’ of Scripture.” (De princ. 4.1.12)

David Steinmetz

“[W]hile all Scripture was given for the edification of the Church and the nurture of the three theological virtues of faith, hope, and love, not all the stories in the Bible are edifying as they stand. What is the spiritual point of the story of the drunkenness of Noah, the murder of Sisera, or the oxgoad of Shamgar, son of Anath? If it cannot be found on the level of narrative, then it must be found on the level of allegory, metaphor, and type.”

David Steinmetz

  • Fourfold sense of Scripture
    • Literal sense
    • Allegorical sense (what the church should believe → faith)
    • Tropological sense (what individuals should do → love)
    • Anagogical sense (what concerns the future → hope)

David Steinmetz on Psalm 137:7–9

“The Psalm became a lament of those who long for the establishment of God’s future kingdom and who are trapped in this disordered and troubled world, which with all its delights is still not their home. They seek an abiding city. The imprecations against the Edomites and the Babylonians are transmuted into condemnations of the world, the flesh, and the devil. If you grant the fourfold sense of scripture, David sings like a Christian.”

The Lutheran Map: Law and Gospel

  • Orienting tool: law vs. gospel
  • Map purpose: to clarify discontinuity between command and promise

Martin Luther (1483–1546)

“The entire Scripture of God is divided into two parts: commandments and promises. Although the commandments teach things that are good, the things taught are not done as soon as they are taught, for the commandments show us what we ought to do but do not give us the power to do it. They are intended to teach man to know himself, that through them he may recognize his inability to do good and may despair of his own ability.”

The Law

  • Consists in anything that is a command, which …
    • Reveals sin (Rom 7:9)
    • Increases sin (Rom 5:20)
    • Restrains evil
    • Shows our need for the Gospel to drive us to Christ

Graham: “Since Luther frequently cites and uses the Old Testament, this does not mean he in any way relegates the law to some forgotten realm. No. Luther rather puts law in its God-given place. It threatens and restrains so that we better understand the Gospel of freedom.”

The Gospel

  • Consists in anything that is a promise.
  • Graham: “The Gospel bestows the Spirit of freedom to the Christian so that we can obey from the heart. The law is not then thrown out but put in its place.”

The Reformed Map: Covenant Theology (Michael Horton)

  • Orienting tool: covenant
  • Map purpose: show the continuity of God’s gracious plan from beginning to end while accounting for discontinuities in administrations of that plan

Michael Horton

“Highlighting both continuity of the one covenant of grace stretching from Genesis 3:15 to Revelation 22:21, covenant theology also recognizes the distinction between covenants based on the principle of law (“Do this and you will live”) and those based on the principle of promise (“I will do this and you will live”). Behind these covenants lies the eternal covenant of redemption… between the persons of the Trinity that provides the foundation for God’s gracious purposes in Christ the Mediator.”

The Dispensational Map: Literal Interpretation

  • Orienting tool: literal interpretation
  • Map purpose: to preserve discontinuity between Israel and the Church, reflecting the diverse ways God may be glorified (but one way of salvation)

Mark Snoeberger

  • Scripture is a history of the rule of God, in which God governs through two governments.
  • Distinction between Israel and the church
    • Israel: both civil and redemptive spheres (united under Moses and David, promises to be fulfilled in Christ in the millenium)
    • Church: civil and redemptive spheres divorced (Matt 22:21); the church is spiritual only

How does the dispensational map (literal interpretation) interpret Isaiah 2:1–5?

Michael Vlach

“What distinguishes all dispensationalists… is belief in a restoration of Israel. The concept of restoration certainly includes the idea of salvation for Israel, but it goes beyond that. ‘Restoration’ involves the idea of Israel being reinstalled as a nation, in her land, with a specific identity and role of service to the nations. In a literal, earthly kingdom—a millennium—the nation Israel will serve a functional role to the nations (Isa. 2:2–4).”

Michael Vlach

“Dispensationalism affirms that Israel is a vehicle for bringing blessings to Gentiles (see Gen. 12:2–3), but it is not God’s intent to make Gentiles part of Israel.
“From Exodus 19 onward the nation Israel was the mediatorial vehicle for God’s purposes in the world. Yet with Israel’s failure, culminating in Israel’s rejection of her own Messiah, the church became God’s vehicle for gospel and kingdom proclamation in this age between the two comings of Jesus. God is still saving a remnant of Israelites (Rom. 11:1–6), but in this age the church is the messenger of God’s kingdom program, taking the gospel to all nations. When God saves the mass of national Israel in the future (Rom. 11:26) Israel will once again have a mediatorial role of service and leadership to the nations under Jesus the Messiah who at that time will be ruling the nations (Isa. 2:2–4; Matt. 19:28; Rev. 19:15). But in this age the church is the primary agent for God’s kingdom purposes.”

    Navigational Tools: Continuity and Discontinuity

    The Issue: What applies to us, and how?

    Introduction to the Concept: Unity and Diversity in the Bible

    “Same same but different”

    • God’s character never changes (same).
    • God’s revelation is progressive (different).
      • Over time, God reveals more.
      • In different admininistrations, God relates to humans in different ways.
    • Jesus is the climax of God’s revelation (Heb 1:1–2) as well as the way to relate to God (John 14:6; Eph 2:18).

    What things are same or different between the Old and New Covenants?

      Old Covenant New Covenant
    Same

    God’s character (holiness, power, wisdom, goodness and love, etc.)

    Different

    		 People: mostly from the descendants of Abraham People: from all nations

    Quick Rules of thumb

    • Easy: Apply promises and obey commands reaffirmed in the NT (e.g., promise of forgiveness, commands to love God and neighbor, not murder, etc.).
    • Somewhat easy: Learn about God’s character and way of relating to humans from the text (ladder of abstraction).
    • A bit harder: Recognizing the differences of administration between the OT and the NT and the centrality of Jesus in God’s plans, transpose the message of the text for our time and place (Israel as a paradigm).

    Type-Antitype

    Introduction

    Definition of Typology

    (Simplified from Gentry and Wellum,
    God’s Kingdom through God’s Covenants, p. 39)

    Typology is a way of interpreting Old Testament patterns (types) that God intended to be previews of their New Testament fulfillment (antitypes) in Jesus or his work, in both his first and second comings.

    • The type (OT pattern) relates organically to the antitype (its NT fulfillment).
    • The type is prophetic, which means God means for and ensures that the type is fulfilled.
    • The type is lesser, the antitype is greater.

    The Benefits and Limits
    of Typology

    • Typology helps us see how the OT anticipates Jesus.
    • Typology helps explain why some elements of the Old Covenant no longer apply.
    • Typology guides us in applying the Old Testament to us.
    • Typology should not be used to dismiss Israel in God’s plans.
    • Typology should not be used to correct the Old Testament.
    • Typology should not be used to make Scripture say what it doesn’t say.
